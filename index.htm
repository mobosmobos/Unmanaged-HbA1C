Poster Overview

Introduction: A Patient-Centered Healthcare delivery model can enhance disease identification and is appropriate for disease prevention and treatment delivery. Periodontitis is a chronic oral disease and given the bidirectional relationship between diabetes/prediabetes and periodontitis, timely referral of patients with diabetes and prediabetes during dental and medical encounters would benefit both patient and health care systems.

The large number of people with diabetes or prediabetes in U.S. necessitates attentive monitoring and referral to decrease morbidity and associated financial burden of these conditions, and to improve health outcomes. The aim of this study was to determine how many adults with elevated (unmanaged) blood glycemic levels and periodontitis could potentially benefit from proper referrals in a fully integrated health-system.

 

Objectives: To determine how many prediabetic, diabetic, and periodontitis patients could potentially benefit from earlier referrals if a fully-integrated medical-dental healthcare system existed.

 

Methods: A total of 6,691 people from NHANES years 2011-14, representing 143M adults, were analyzed. RStudio with appropriate packages used for data wrangling and delivering final analytical dataset. SAS statistical software used to finalize statistical analysis for both Bivariate and Multivariate analysis.

 

Results:

The prevalence of periodontitis, diabetes, and prediabetes were 53.4M (37.3%), 12.2M (8.5%), and 50M (34.8%), respectively. A total of 39.4M undiagnosed prediabetics and 2.4M undiagnosed diabetics were identified.

 

Among the population with a DV in the past year but no MV, 2.3M were prediabetic (2.1M undiagnosed) and 521,000 were diabetic (a total of 363,000 undiagnosed).

 

Among the population with a MV but no DV, there were 17.7M people with periodontitis, which 7.4M were prediabetic (73.9% undiagnosed) and 3M diabetic (10% undiagnosed).

 

If dental providers could identify undiagnosed prediabetics and diabetics with periodontitis and medical providers could identify those with periodontitis, we estimate that up to 20M people could be referred for medical-dental follow-up.

 

More than half of the US population has unmanaged HbA1C levels (62.1 M). There were (2.4 M) undiagnosed diabetics, (39.3 M) undiagnosed prediabetics, and (53.5 M) adults with periodontitis.

 

Among the population with a MV in the past year but no DV, there were (9.4 M) diagnosed diabetics, (6.1 M) diagnosed prediabetics, and (1 M) undiagnosed diabetics and (22.7 M) undiagnosed prediabetics. The number of adults estimated to have periodontitis and had a medical visit but no dental visit in the past year was (17.7 M).

Among the population with a DV in the past year but no MV, there were (0.25 M) diagnosed diabetics, (0.34 M) diagnosed prediabetics, (0.36 M) undiagnosed diabetics and (2.1 M) undiagnosed prediabetics.

 

Adults with periodontitis were more than twice as likely to have unmanaged HbA1C compared to healthy individuals (OR=2.30, p>0.0001).

Adults with a recent MV but no DV were 30% more likely to have unmanaged HbA1C compared to adults with recent MV and DV, controlling for sociodemographic factors and medical insurance.

 

Unmanaged HbA1C levels and periodontitis were more pronounce among older adults (affecting 17.2 M and 13.7 M respectively), and non-Hispanic blacks (5.7 M and 7.7 M respectively).
